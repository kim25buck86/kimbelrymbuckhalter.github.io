## About My Project

My DREAM Apprenticeship research project is titled "Expanding the Future Computing Workforce Through Computer Science Education Pathways in Mississippi."
This project examines how computer science education pathways can contribute to developing Mississippi's future computing workforce. Through a review of existing literature and available data, the study explores participation, access, and success in computer science education across the state. The research focuses on factors such as race, ethnicity, gender, geographic location, and educational opportunities that may influence student participation in computer science. The goal is to identify challenges, opportunities, and strategies that can strengthen computer science education pathways and support a more diverse and prepared computing workforce in Mississippi.

# Week 2: Gender Representation in Computer Science: Challenges and Opportunities

As I begin my DREAM research project, I wanted to first explore one of the most frequently discussed topics in computer science education: the underrepresentation of women in computing. Although computer science is one of the fastest-growing fields in the United States, women continue to be significantly underrepresented in both educational programs and technology careers.

My initial literature review revealed that women have historically played important roles in computing. Early computer programming relied heavily on women, and many women made significant contributions to the development of modern computing technologies. However, over time, cultural stereotypes and changing perceptions of the profession contributed to a decline in female participation in computer science (Nowak, 2024). Women who were once central to the field became increasingly underrepresented as computing became viewed as a male-dominated profession.

Several studies identified barriers that continue to affect women's participation in computer science today. Common challenges include gender stereotypes, bias, microaggressions, and a lack of representation in educational and professional settings. These factors can negatively impact confidence, sense of belonging, and persistence within the field (Kuzerbayeva, Sahin, & Sathya, 2024).

Another important theme that emerged from the literature was the importance of community and support networks. Research from Northwestern University found that women-in-computing organizations can help students develop confidence, build a sense of belonging, and strengthen their identity as computer science students. These groups provide supportive environments that encourage women to remain engaged in computing education and careers. The study also noted that women's representation among computer science degree recipients declined from more than 35% in the 1980s to approximately 21.9% in 2021.

Additional research highlighted how universities are working to improve female participation in computing and data science programs. Boston University reported that women make up only about 15–20% of data science professionals nationally, yet intentional recruitment and retention efforts have helped increase female enrollment in their computing programs. These findings suggest that institutions can play an important role in creating pathways that attract and support women in computing fields.

Finally, research examining computer systems conferences found that women represented only about 10% of researchers in that area of computer science. The authors concluded that increasing participation will require addressing deeper systemic issues rather than relying solely on diversity initiatives.

Overall, the literature suggests that increasing female participation in computer science requires more than recruitment efforts alone. Building supportive communities, addressing stereotypes, providing mentorship, and creating accessible educational pathways may help broaden participation in computing.

While the studies reviewed in this blog focus primarily on national trends, they provide an important foundation for understanding gender representation in computer science. As this research project continues, I will explore how these trends compare to opportunities and challenges within Mississippi. Understanding the experiences of women in computing at both the national and state levels may help identify strategies for expanding participation in graduate computer science education and strengthening Mississippi's future computing workforce.
